What is the Real Problem?

The Real Problem: Roads Without Mobility

India's PMGSY programme achieved near-total rural road coverage. Yet for 86 crore rural Indians, having a road nearby doesn't mean they can actually go anywhere — because there's no bus, no affordable transport, and no last-mile connection.

Road built, no bus arrived
63% urban buses

Maharashtra Bus Paradox

63% of Maharashtra's state buses run in cities. Only 37% serve rural areas where 55% of people live. (ITDP 2026)

One-third of India's villages lose road access during monsoon season each year, cutting off farmers, patients, and students. (World Bank)

🚌 THE BUS PARADOX

India's rural areas have the roads — but not the buses. State transport corporations, financially stressed, focus on profitable urban routes. Rural buses are few, infrequent, and overcrowded. Where private operators fill the gap, fares are high and safety is poor.

8,700 /14,000

Maharashtra Urban Bus Bias

8,700 of 14,000 state buses run urban routes. Rural Maharashtra — home to 55% of people — gets only 5,300. (ITDP 2026)

1 bus per 2 hrs

Typical Rural Frequency

Many rural routes in states like UP, Bihar, MP see only 1–2 bus services per day. Waiting hours is common.

Jeep Chakda Kaduka

Para-Transit Reality

Jeeps, chakdas and kadukas fill rural gaps — but are unregulated, unsafe, and expensive. (CPPR / Wikipedia)

What Are the Challenges?

Key Challenges for Rural India

India's rural transport crisis is multi-layered. Physical roads exist — but services, safety, affordability, and inclusion remain deeply broken across most of rural India.

🚌 No Bus, No Transport

State road transport corporations run deficits and abandon rural routes. In Maharashtra 63% of buses are urban. In MP, MPSRTC's dissolution halted rural services entirely, leaving villages dependent on costly, unsafe private vehicles.

Service Absence

🌧️ Monsoon Cut-Off

33% of villages lose road access during monsoon every year. Unpaved, poorly drained roads become impassable. Pregnant women, patients, and farmers are stranded — this is a life-and-death infrastructure failure.

Seasonal Crisis

👩 Women's Mobility Denied

Rural women travel far less than men — 88% of men travel 1km+ vs 76% women. The gap grows at longer distances. Transport poverty affects women's education, healthcare access and economic participation most severely.

Gender Inequity

🌾 Farmers Lose Produce

Without timely, affordable transport, perishable crops rot before reaching mandis. Farmers sell to middlemen at low prices because they can't self-transport. Road connectivity without market transport service is half a solution.

Agricultural Impact

⚖ Broken Regulations

Rigid licensing designed for large fleet operators blocks small-vehicle entrepreneurs — jeeps, tempos, e-rickshaws — from legally serving rural routes. Informal operators exist everywhere, but without legal status, safety, or fair fares.

Policy Failure

🏥 Healthcare Unreachable

Rural CHCs face 79.5% shortfall of specialists. When a doctor is even available, patients can't reach the facility. In eastern and northeastern India, both affordability and proximity barriers remain acute for women seeking healthcare.

Health Access

📚 Education Dropout

Girls in rural India drop out of secondary education disproportionately due to transport barriers. Safe, affordable transport to colleges is nearly absent. Travel to higher education is a documented barrier to rural women's advancement.

Education

💰 Maintenance Neglect

India spends the bulk of transport budgets on new construction. Road maintenance is chronic underfunded. PMGSY roads built in early phases are now degrading — without upkeep, the ₹7.8 lakh km investment will erode within years.

Infrastructure

Government Schemes

Government Schemes & Programmes

India's central and state governments have launched multiple schemes to address rural transport. Here's what exists, what it covers, and where the gaps remain.

🛣️

PMGSY – Pradhan Mantri Gram Sadak Yojana

Prime Minister's Village Road Scheme

India's flagship rural road programme. Connects habitations with all-weather roads. Currently in Phase IV (2024–2029) targeting 25,000 remaining habitations with 62,500 km roads.

₹70,125 Cr
🏕️

PM-JANMAN – Tribal Connectivity

Tribal Justice Great Campaign

Special PMGSY vertical for Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs). Targets 8,000 km for hamlets with even fewer than 100 residents. 5,718 km already sanctioned.

₹4,366 Cr
🚌

FAME-II & EV Bus Push

Electric Bus Scheme

Subsidies for electric buses under FAME-II scheme — but largely focused on cities. Rural e-bus pilots exist in Tamil Nadu where fares dropped by 15%, offering a model to scale.

15% ↓ fares
👩

Shakti Scheme (Karnataka)

Empowerment Scheme

Free bus travel for women in Karnataka. Nationwide ridership grew 8% in 2026, partly driven by Shakti. A powerful model showing that removing fare barriers increases rural women's mobility.

Free for women
🏥

Ayushman Bharat + Mobile Clinics

Mobile Health Clinic

Where patients can't reach hospitals, mobile health clinics bring healthcare to villages. Addresses the last-mile healthcare gap when transport infrastructure is absent.

Health to village
🛤️

Bharatmala Pariyojana

Bharatmala Highway Project

National highway expansion programme. While focused on highways, improved state highway access feeds rural feeder routes. India now builds 30+ km of road per day vs 12–14 km pre-2014.

30+ km/day

What Are the Solutions?

Evidence-Based Solutions for Indian Villages

Recommendations grounded in research from CPPR, PMGSY, MoSPI, World Bank, and field reports across rural India. Not theory — proven, scalable interventions.

1

🚐 Legalise & Support Small-Vehicle Rural Entrepreneurs

Reform permit and licensing laws to formally recognise jeeps, tempos, e-rickshaws, and mini-buses on rural routes. Informal operators already exist — they need legal status, route guarantees, safety training, and access to affordable credit for vehicle purchase. CPPR research identifies this as the fastest path to rural mobility.

Source: CPPR Rural Mobility Report, February 2026
2

👩 Scale Shakti Model Nationally — Free/Subsidised Buses for Women

Karnataka's Shakti scheme proved that removing fare barriers dramatically increases rural women's mobility and economic participation. A national rollout, even partially subsidised, would address the gender mobility gap (88% vs 76% travel rates) and unlock rural women's access to education, markets, and healthcare.

Source: indiadatamap 2026, Springer Annals 2026
3

🌧️ Monsoon-Proof Road Design (Drain First, Tar Second)

33% of India's villages lose connectivity every monsoon. PMGSY-IV should mandate drainage-first construction standards, culvert placement, and embankment protection. Green tech roads (waste plastic, Cold Mix, FDR) being piloted must become standard — 15,783 km was built this way in 2024 alone.

Source: PIB India 2026, World Bank
4

🔧 Fix Maintenance: Shift 25% of PMGSY Budget to Upkeep

India builds roads, then neglects them. Only ₹4,056 crore was spent on maintenance vs tens of thousands of crores on construction. A mandatory 20–25% maintenance allocation in all rural road budgets would protect the existing 7.8 lakh km network from degradation.

Source: PIB PMGSY eMARG data 2026
5

📱 Digital Rural Route Planning — App-Based Bus Tracking

State RTCs should provide real-time bus tracking and route information for rural services. Bihar's short-distance bus network showed 90% bus dependency for low-income riders — giving them timetable clarity reduces missed connections and wasted waiting time.

Source: indiadatamap 2026
6

⚡ Prioritise Rural E-Bus Corridors in FAME-III

Tamil Nadu's e-bus pilot reduced fares by 15% — showing rural e-buses are economically viable when supported. FAME-III should ring-fence a share of EV subsidies for rural route operators, prioritising states like Bihar, UP, MP, and Odisha where rural bus networks are most depleted.

Source: indiadatamap 2026, IBEF 2024
7

🏥 Transport + Health Integration: Village Health Transport Vouchers

Issue transport vouchers to pregnant women and patients under Ayushman Bharat. Proximity was the most improved but still largest barrier for women accessing healthcare (56.79% faced it in 2021, down from 63.2% in 2016). Bridging the last-mile with subsidised transport directly saves lives.

Source: BMC Public Health / NFHS data, 2026
